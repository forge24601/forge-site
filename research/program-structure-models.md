## 1. Program Structure Models

### Cohort Models vs. Rolling Enrollment

Most successful prison programs use a **cohort model** rather than rolling enrollment. Cohorts create peer accountability, shared identity, and allow structured progression through curriculum phases.

- **PEP (Texas)**: Runs fixed cohorts through a 4-month in-prison mini-MBA. Participants are transferred to specific TDCJ units (Oliver J. Bell or Sanders Estes) for the program. Over 6,000 men across 80 TDCJ units are invited annually via correspondence coursework; qualified completers advance to in-person cohorts.
- **Defy Ventures**: 7-month cohort-based CEO of Your New Life (CEO YNL) program. EITs apply and are accepted into defined classes.
- **Bard Prison Initiative**: Semester-based academic cohorts following a traditional college enrollment model with competitive admissions. 400 students enrolled full-time across seven NY prisons.
- **RSVP (San Francisco)**: Uses a hybrid model -- a revolving/rolling system where longer-tenured participants mentor incoming participants. 44 inmates live together in one dedicated housing unit. New inmates learn from experienced men who are nearing completion.
- **Hope for Prisoners (Las Vegas)**: Begins with a week-long prevocational workshop cohort, then 18 months of individualized mentoring.

**Key insight**: Cohort models are preferred for intensive transformation programs because they build trust, shared vocabulary, and mutual accountability. Rolling enrollment works better for ongoing support and when the peer-mentoring-from-senior-participants model is central to the design (as in RSVP).

### Phase/Tier Systems

The most effective programs use clear multi-phase progression:

**PEP (Texas) -- 3 Phases:**
1. **Leadership and Accountability Curriculum (LACC)**: Distance-education correspondence coursework. Multi-module self-reflection. Entry screening.
2. **In-Prison Mini-MBA (4 months)**: Business plan development, 40+ business exams, 200+ business pitches to executives, pro-forma income statements. Culminates in 30-minute oral business plan presentation to CEO/VC judging panel. Cap-and-gown graduation.
3. **Post-Release Reentry**: Transitional housing, transportation, family liaisons. eSchool 101 (16-20 workshops over 7 months). eSchool 201 (advanced track for actual business launch).

**Defy Ventures -- 4 Tiers:**
1. **CEO of Your New Life**: 7-month foundational program. 1,200 pages of proprietary curriculum. Employment readiness, healthy habits, entrepreneurship. Trauma-informed. Video faculty includes formerly incarcerated individuals, Harvard/Stanford professors, VCs, career coaches. Culminates in Shark Tank-style pitch competition and graduation.
2. **Entrepreneur Bootcamp**: Alternative entry for those who missed the in-prison program. Comparable training post-release.
3. **Business Accelerator**: For EITs who have achieved stable housing and employment. Business incorporation, launch, and growth support.
4. **Alumni Programs**: Technology workshops, resume support, interview prep, individual action plans, peer network.

**Bard Prison Initiative -- Academic Tiers:**
1. **Associate Degree (AA)**: Foundational sequence -- First-Year Seminar, Grammar/Rhetoric/Style, Citizen Science, Intro to Cultural Anthropology. Courses across all four academic divisions.
2. **Bachelor's Degree (BA)**: Competitive admission to upper college. Advanced seminars, moderation into major, senior thesis.
3. **Master's Degree (MA)**: Pilot program for BA graduates. MA in Public Humanities at Eastern NY Correctional Facility.

**Georgia DOC -- Cognitive Programming Tiers:**
- **Offenders Under Transition (O.U.T.)**: 200-hour cognitive-behavioral program in three modules. Designed to address criminal thinking and build pro-social skills.
- **Reentry Centers**: 12 centers statewide, 2,344 transitional beds. ~6 months of work release before parole.
- **Ready4Reentry Forensic Peer Mentor Training**: Specialized track to train people with lived experience to support others in reentry.

### Session Frequency and Duration

Evidence-based practices indicate programs should be tailored to risk level:

| Program | Schedule | Duration |
|---------|----------|----------|
| RSVP (San Francisco) | 10 hours/day, 5 days/week (50 hrs/wk) | Minimum 4 months |
| PEP (Texas) in-prison | Full-time immersive | 4 months |
| PEP eSchool 101 | Weekly workshops | 7 months (16-20 sessions) |
| Defy Ventures CEO YNL | Regular classes + peer discussion + events | 7 months |
| BPI (Bard) | Full-time college courseload | 2-4+ years (degree programs) |
| Hope for Prisoners | Week-long intensive + ongoing | 18 months total |
| From Prison Cells to PhD | Weekend workshops + weekly mentoring | 2 months intensive + 10 months mentoring |
| CDCR OMCP | Full-time training | ~1 year education + 2,000 internship hours |

**Federal BOP examples** (for comparison):
- Circle of Strength: 13 sessions, 1.5 hours/week
- Dialectical Behavior Therapy: 52 sessions, 1.5-2 hours/week
- Sex Offender Treatment: Multiple sessions/week

**Best practice**: High-risk individuals need multimodal, longer-term, more intensive services. Low-risk individuals need minimal exposure.

### Graduation/Certification Requirements

- **PEP**: Pass 40+ business exams, pitch business concept 200+ times, deliver 30-minute oral business plan to CEO/VC panel. Cap-and-gown ceremony inside prison.
- **Defy Ventures**: Complete 1,200 pages of curriculum, create resume/personal statement/business concept plan, compete in Shark Tank-style pitch competition. Certificate of completion and cap-and-gown graduation.
- **BPI**: Complete full Bard College degree requirements (AA, BA, or MA). Accredited Bard College degrees.
- **CDCR OMCP**: Complete ~1 year of AOD coursework, pass California State-recognized AOD certification exam, fulfill 255 hours practicum, accumulate 2,000+ supervised internship hours. Earn recognized AOD Counselor certification.
- **PEP eSchool 101**: Complete 16-20 workshops and assignments with passing grades within 7 months.

### How Programs Handle Dropouts and Accountability

Research indicates attrition rates range from **20% (prison-based adult programs) to 60% (inpatient juvenile programs)**.

**Key distinctions in non-completion:**
- **Dropouts**: Leave due to personal interest/motivation or socio-demographic factors
- **Discharges**: Removed due to behavioral infractions or institutional processes

**Consequences of dropping out:**
- Elevated reoffending risk
- Loss of motivation for behavioral change
- Potential stigmatization within the justice system
- Negative impact on staff motivation and program climate

**Best practices for managing attrition:**
1. **Second chances**: Allow "failed" participants to reapply. Program failure can be a learning experience that increases motivation.
2. **Early intervention**: Anticipate and address attrition/noncompliance risks early in the treatment process.
3. **Program quality audits**: Use tools like the Correctional Program Checklist to assess fidelity of implementation.
4. **Incentive systems**: Recognize small accomplishments. Offer tangible rewards for adherence.
5. **Peer accountability**: Programs like RSVP use peer pressure and community accountability as primary retention mechanisms.

## 2. Successful Programs Outside California

### Prison Entrepreneurship Program (PEP) -- Texas

- **Location**: Cleveland Correctional Facility and other TDCJ units
- **Founded**: 2004
- **Scale**: 1,000+ individuals served annually; 3,500+ graduates total
- **Structure**: 3-phase model (distance education screening, 4-month in-prison MBA, post-release reentry services)
- **Curriculum**: Certified by Baylor University. Business plan development, financial modeling, executive mentoring, leadership development across 15 topic areas.
- **Mentor model**: Participants "help facilitate the lessons they learned to the next class behind them" (servant leadership model). 90% of PEP staff are program graduates.
- **Volunteer engagement**: 128 participants per class, 211 servant volunteers (business executives, CEOs, MBAs)
- **Post-release**: eSchool 101/201, The Collider (business incubator), Entre Capital (funding), Family Restoration services
- **Outcomes**: Significant recidivism reduction (see ICIC impact analysis)

**Sources:**
- [PEP Official Site](https://www.pep.org/)
- [What We Do](https://www.pep.org/what-we-do/)
- [ICIC Impact Analysis](https://icic.org/blog/innovative-prison-entrepreneurship-program-creating-wealth-reducing-recidivism-among-formerly-incarcerated/)
- [AEI Report](https://www.aei.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/12/Prison-Entrepreneurship-Program.pdf)

### Defy Ventures -- Multi-State (8 states)

- **Headquarters**: Los Angeles
- **Core program**: CEO of Your New Life (CEO YNL) -- 7 months, 1,200 pages proprietary curriculum
- **Language**: No "convicts" or "inmates" -- only "Entrepreneurs-in-Training" (EITs)
- **Curriculum blend**: Job readiness, entrepreneurship, tech basics, personal finance, etiquette, character development, re-entry planning. Trauma-informed.
- **Faculty**: Formerly incarcerated individuals, entrepreneurs, VCs, Harvard/Stanford professors, career coaches
- **Pitch competition**: Shark Tank-style business pitch. EITs must also publicly discuss past transgressions ("leading with accountability").
- **Business requirements**: Customer problem identification, MVP development, overhead cost planning, realistic scaling pathway.
- **4-tier progression**: CEO YNL -> Entrepreneur Bootcamp -> Business Accelerator -> Alumni Programs
- **Outcomes**: 85% employed within 6 months of release; <10% one-year recidivism rate (vs. 40%+ national average)

**Sources:**
- [Defy Ventures Programs](https://www.defyventures.org/our-story-main/our-programs)
- [Fortune Article (Feb 2026)](https://fortune.com/2026/02/20/defy-ventures-teaches-entrepreneurship-in-prisons-changed-how-i-think-about-founders/)
- [Chicago Reporter](https://www.chicagoreporter.com/redefining-reentry-how-defy-ventures-illinois-empowers-entrepreneurs-with-empathy/)

### Bard Prison Initiative (BPI) -- New York

- **Parent institution**: Bard College
- **Scale**: 400+ students full-time across 7 NY state prisons
- **Model**: Full accredited college education inside prisons -- AA, BA, and MA degrees from Bard College
- **Academic divisions**: Arts; Languages/Literature/Humanities; Science/Math/Computing; Social Studies
- **Admissions**: Competitive application process
- **Progression**: Foundational sequence (First-Year Seminar, Grammar/Rhetoric/Style, Citizen Science) -> AA degree -> BA application/moderation into major/senior thesis -> MA (pilot at Eastern NY)
- **Peer tutoring**: Students take pedagogy courses, then serve as peer tutors. 50+ students have participated as peer tutors in affiliated programs.
- **Reentry**: Planning begins upon enrollment. Comprehensive support in continuing education, employment, housing, technology, and wellness.
- **Alumni network**: Formal mutual support and peer mentoring network throughout NY
- **National impact**: Established Consortium for Liberal Arts in Prison. Bard Microcolleges for formerly incarcerated students.

**Sources:**
- [BPI Official Site](https://bpi.bard.edu/)
- [College-in-Prison](https://bpi.bard.edu/college-in-prison-and-beyond/college-in-prison/)
- [PMC Public Health Article](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6987918/)

### Resolve to Stop the Violence Project (RSVP) -- San Francisco

- **Location**: San Francisco County Jail #7, San Bruno
- **Founded by**: Sunny Schwartz (SF Sheriff's Department) with Hamish Sinclair (ManAlive)
- **Structure**: Intensive 50-hour/week program (10 hours/day, 5 days/week). Minimum 4-month participation.
- **Housing**: Dedicated housing unit -- 44 participants live together
- **Curriculum components**:
  - ManAlive Violence Intervention Program (theoretical foundation): Gender-based analysis of violence. Unlearning "Male Role Belief System" of superiority/dominance. Emotional literacy and intimacy education.
  - Group discussions and counseling sessions
  - Academic classes through Five Keys Charter Schools
  - Theatrical enactments and role-playing
  - Art and creative writing
  - Victim/survivor impact discussions
- **Peer mentoring**: Revolving model -- experienced participants guide newer inmates. Peer facilitators are inmates who have completed or are in final stages of the program.
- **Accountability**: Mandatory attendance. Dense schedule leaves no idle time. Peer pressure and community accountability.
- **Restorative justice**: Brings together victims, communities, and offenders. Focus on victim restoration, offender accountability, and community involvement.
- **Outcomes**: Up to 80% reduction in recidivism for violent rearrests.

**Sources:**
- [RSVP on Restorative Justice](https://restorativejustice.org/rj-archive/resolve-to-stop-the-violence-project-stopping-violence-from-the-inside-out-part-i/)
- [Community Works West](https://communityworkswest.org/our-work/transformation/)
- [PubMed Study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15820997/)
- [IIRP - Dreams from the Monster Factory](https://www.iirp.edu/news/dreams-from-the-monster-factory-a-restorative-prison-program-for-violent-offenders)

### Friends of Island Academy / Youth Justice Network -- NYC (Rikers)

- **Founded**: 1990 by educators and advocates at the alternative high school on Rikers Island
- **Renamed**: Youth Justice Network (2021)
- **Scale**: 1,000+ justice-involved young people annually; network connects ~3,000 upon admission
- **Model**: Comprehensive intake-through-reentry system
  - Advocates meet young people from first moments in custody
  - Work with them throughout incarceration
  - Build support network for return to neighborhoods
- **Education**: 9:1 student-teacher ratio with individualized tutoring and advocacy support
- **Funding**: $11.5M pilot contract from NYC Department of Correction for city-wide Youth Reentry Network
- **Reentry focus**: Discharge planning and community-based support

**Sources:**

### Credible Messenger Programs -- NYC

While a specific program called "HALT Violence" was not found in searches, NYC operates a significant ecosystem of credible messenger violence intervention programs:

**Institute for Transformative Mentoring (ITM) at The New School:**
- Semester-long, college-level course for formerly incarcerated credible messengers working in social services
- Curriculum: Trauma-informed care, youth development, history of mass incarceration/social justice, career advancement
- Methods: Activities, role-play, lesson plan development, restorative justice practices
- Outcomes: 3 college credits earned. 97.7% completion rate. 75% earned A grades. 90%+ attendance.
- Summer Youth Intensive: Condensed version for mentees interested in becoming peer mentors (expanded to year-long engagement in 2018)

**Credible Messenger Mentoring Movement (CM3):**
- Network supporting credible messenger programs across NYC
- Focuses on training formerly incarcerated individuals as mentors for at-risk youth

**Advance Peace Model (adopted in NYC):**
- 18-month mentoring and life-planning cycle
- Fellows receive ~$1,000/month stipend plus bonuses for meeting life goals (GED, driver's license, etc.)

**Impact**: 57% decrease in convictions among young people working with credible messenger mentors (Urban Institute evaluation).

## 3. Mentor Development Pipelines

### How Programs Develop Participants INTO Mentors

**Model 1: PEP "Servant Leadership" Pipeline**
- **Progression**: During the in-prison program, participants begin facilitating lessons to the next cohort behind them. They practice teaching/mentoring skills across 15 leadership topic areas while still in the program.
- **Staff pipeline**: 90% of PEP staff are program graduates -- creating a full pipeline from participant to employee.
- **Timeline**: Begins during the 4-month in-prison program; staff roles come post-release.

**Model 2: Hope for Prisoners "Mentornship" Pipeline**
- **Phase 1**: 18-month mentoring program as a client (week-long prevocational workshop + ongoing mentoring with case management)
- **Phase 2**: After 1 year of mentoring, clients are invited to a 6-month "mentornship" training program
- **Progression**: Trained mentornship graduates become volunteer mentors, giving back by supporting new clients
- **Mentor teams**: Each mentor is part of a team matched by demographics and life experiences. 200+ volunteer mentors. 125+ Las Vegas police officers also participate.

**Model 3: CDCR Occupational Mentor Certification Program (OMCP)**
- **Eligibility**: 5+ years remaining on sentence (including lifers). No serious disciplinary infractions in 2+ years. Minimum 9th-grade reading level.
- **Phase 1 -- CBI Training**: 18 weeks of Cognitive Behavioral Interventions
- **Phase 2 -- AOD Education**: 6 months including orientation, study prep, 350 hours classroom education on 12 core functions of AOD counseling, 255 hours practicum
- **Phase 3 -- Internship**: 2,000+ supervised hours as paid co-facilitator in CBI programs across CA institutions
- **Certification**: California State-recognized AOD Counselor certification
- **Compensation**: Paid position. Earns milestone credit and educational merit credit toward sentence.
- **Timeline**: ~1 year education + extended internship period
- **Locations**: 7 California institutions

**Model 4: RSVP Peer Facilitator Model**
- **Progression**: Organic -- experienced participants naturally mentor newer inmates in the shared housing unit
- **Facilitator role**: Inmates who have completed or are in final stages become small-group facilitators
- **No formal certification**: The revolving model relies on immersion and demonstrated behavioral change
- **Quality control**: Dense daily schedule (10 hrs/day) and peer pressure create continuous accountability

**Model 5: Vera Institute Restoring Promise**
- **Eligibility**: Incarcerated adults age 25+ serving longer sentences
- **Role**: Coach young adults (18-25) on conflict resolution, financial literacy, relationships, and essential life skills
- **Structure**: Dedicated housing units where mentors live alongside mentees
- **Scale**: 9 young adult housing units in 6 states since 2016

**Model 6: BPI Peer Tutoring Pipeline**
- **Progression**: Students take a pedagogy course -> serve as peer tutors -> support other incarcerated people's educational pursuits
- **Scale**: 50+ students have served as peer tutors in affiliated programs
- **Alumni**: Network provides ongoing mutual support and peer mentoring post-release

**Model 7: ITM Credible Messenger Development**
- **Entry**: Must be formerly incarcerated and working in social services
- **Training**: Semester-long college-level course at The New School
- **Competencies**: Trauma-informed care, youth development, social justice framework, career advancement
- **Credential**: 3 college credits from The New School
- **Youth pathway**: Summer Youth Intensive for mentees interested in becoming peer mentors (expanded to year-long)

**Model 8: From Prison Cells to PhD**
- **Mentor qualifications**: Must be formerly incarcerated college graduates
- **Scholar support**: 2+ years of group and individual mentoring
- **8 core competencies developed**: Critical thinking, oral/written communication, teamwork, digital technology, leadership, professionalism, career management, global/intercultural fluency
- **Mentor training**: Provided by Master's degree holders, certified K-12 educators, and certified career counselors with 30+ years experience

### Common Patterns in Mentor Development

1. **Minimum time before mentoring**: Typically 6-18 months of program participation before a participant can mentor others
2. **Demonstrated competencies**: Behavioral change, subject mastery, emotional regulation, teaching ability
3. **Formal vs. informal**: Programs range from highly certified (OMCP) to organic peer models (RSVP)
4. **Quality maintenance**: Ongoing supervision, team structures, periodic training, and peer accountability

## 4. Program Governance Inside Prisons

### How Incarcerated-Led Programs Maintain Structure

**RSVP Model -- Peer-Led Daily Operations:**
- Senior participants (longest-tenured) guide new inmates
- Facilitators are inmates who run small group sessions
- Dense 50-hour/week schedule eliminates idle time and reduces opportunities for negative behavior
- Mandatory attendance -- no opt-out culture
- ManAlive curriculum provides consistent theoretical framework
- Partnership with SF Sheriff's Department ensures institutional backing

**PEP Model -- Servant Leadership:**
- Participants actively teach and facilitate for the next class
- Creates culture where leadership is demonstrated through service
- Prison administration transfers eligible participants to specific units, showing institutional buy-in

**Vera Restoring Promise -- Dedicated Housing Units:**
- Incarcerated mentors (25+) and young adult mentees live together in redesigned housing units
- Correctional officers are partners, not adversaries
- Structured daily schedules replace traditional idle time
- Mentors help create and maintain unit culture of dignity and accountability

**BPI -- Academic Community:**
- Students create academic culture within prison setting
- Peer tutoring creates informal student governance
- Student-led initiatives support incoming and parenting students
- Alumni network extends governance beyond prison walls

### Leadership Roles and Responsibilities

Common roles observed across programs:
- **Peer facilitators/mentors**: Lead small group discussions, model behavior, orient new participants
- **Teaching assistants**: Support curriculum delivery (PEP, BPI)
- **Unit representatives**: Interface between participants and administration
- **Alumni leaders**: Maintain community connections and support network post-release

### Accountability Systems

1. **Peer pressure and community accountability** (RSVP, Restoring Promise): Living together in dedicated units creates 24/7 accountability
2. **Academic standards** (BPI, PEP): Grades, exams, and presentations serve as objective measures
3. **Behavioral requirements** (OMCP): No serious disciplinary infractions for 2+ years to maintain eligibility
4. **Progress benchmarks** (Defy, PEP): Completion of specific deliverables (business plans, resumes, personal statements)
5. **Graduated consequences**: Most programs distinguish between struggling participants (offered support) and rule violators (may be removed)

### Relationship with Prison Administration

**Critical success factors:**
- Programs that work WITH administration rather than around them show greatest sustainability
- RSVP: Direct partnership with SF Sheriff's Department. Sunny Schwartz was a department administrator.
- PEP: TDCJ transfers eligible participants to PEP-specific units -- deep institutional integration
- BPI: Operates across 7 NY DOC facilities with full institutional cooperation
- OMCP: Created BY CDCR -- fully embedded in the corrections system
- Vera Restoring Promise: Partners directly with corrections leaders to redesign housing units

## 5. Metrics and Assessment

### How Programs Measure Participant Growth

**Beyond recidivism -- alternative outcome measures:**
- Employment rates and speed of employment (Defy: 85% employed within 6 months)
- Housing stability
- Family reunification
- Mental health and well-being
- Educational attainment (BPI: accredited degrees)
- Business creation and revenue (PEP, Defy)
- Substance use recovery
- Violent incident reduction (RSVP: 80% reduction in violent rearrests)
- Conviction reduction (Credible Messengers: 57% decrease)

### Pre/Post Assessment Tools Used

**Standardized instruments:**
- **Social Skills Scale**: Measures interpersonal competency changes
- **Perceived Emotional Intelligence Scale (TMMS-24)**: Pre/post emotional intelligence measurement
- **Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale (RSES)**: Self-esteem changes
- **Cognitive Emotion Regulation Questionnaire (CERQ)**: 64 items measuring 9 cognitive strategies (self-blame, acceptance, rumination, positive refocusing, positive reappraisal, putting into perspective, catastrophizing, blaming others, refocus on planning)
- **Level of Service/Case Management Inventory (LS/CMI)**: Gauges individual risks and criminogenic needs (widely used in US and Canada)

**Risk-need assessment domains** (12 standard areas):
1. Anger/Hostility
2. Antisocial Peers
3. Cognitions
4. Education
5. Family/Parenting
6. Finance/Poverty
7. Medical
8. Mental Health
9. Recreation/Leisure/Fitness
10. Substance Use
11. Trauma
12. Work

**Generation of assessment tools:**
- 3rd generation: Empirically-based and theory-driven
- 4th generation: Systematically link assessment results to treatment plans and case management strategies

### Portfolio and Competency-Based Assessment Models

**PEP competency markers:**
- 40+ business exams passed
- 200+ business pitches delivered
- 30-minute oral business plan presentation
- Pro-forma income statements completed
- eSchool: 16-20 workshops completed with passing grades

**Defy Ventures deliverables portfolio:**
- Resume
- Personal statement
- Business concept plan (with customer problem, MVP, overhead costs, scaling pathway)
- Pitch competition performance

**BPI academic portfolio:**
- Course grades across 4 academic divisions
- Senior thesis (for BA)
- Peer tutoring experience

**OMCP certification portfolio:**
- 350 hours classroom education documented
- 255 hours practicum documented
- 2,000+ supervised internship hours logged
- State certification exam passed

**From Prison Cells to PhD -- 8 core competencies:**
1. Critical thinking and problem-solving
2. Oral and written communications
3. Teamwork and collaboration
4. Digital technology proficiency
5. Leadership capabilities
6. Professionalism and work ethic
7. Career management and self-advocacy
8. Global and intercultural fluency

### Key Performance Measures (California LAO recommendations)

- Participation rates for all state-funded rehabilitation programs
- Length of participation before release
- Progress measurements at defined intervals
- Time needed to meet specified benchmarks
- Completion rates
- Post-release outcome tracking

### Program Quality Assessment

The **Correctional Program Checklist (CPC)** evaluates whether programs:
- Deliver evidence-based interventions with fidelity
- Target appropriate risk levels
- Address criminogenic needs
- Use cognitive-behavioral approaches
- Maintain qualified staff
- Track outcomes systematically

Programs scoring high on the CPC are significantly more likely to reduce recidivism.

## Key Takeaways for Forge

1. **Use a cohort model** with defined phases. The most transformative programs (PEP, Defy, RSVP) use intensive cohorts rather than rolling enrollment. Consider a hybrid where senior cohort members mentor incoming participants.

2. **Build a clear tier system**: Participant -> Advanced Participant -> Peer Mentor -> Facilitator/Staff. PEP's "servant leadership" model (participants teach the next class) and OMCP's formal certification pipeline are the strongest models for developing mentors from within.

3. **Intensity matters**: The most effective programs operate at high intensity -- RSVP at 50 hours/week, PEP at full-time immersive. Programs with occasional weekly sessions show less transformative impact.

4. **Tangible milestones drive engagement**: Business plans, pitch competitions, degrees, certifications, and portfolios give participants concrete evidence of growth and credentials that transfer to the outside world.

5. **Peer accountability is more effective than top-down control**: RSVP's peer-pressure model and PEP's servant leadership create self-sustaining accountability cultures. Dedicated housing units (RSVP, Vera Restoring Promise) amplify this effect.

6. **Administration partnership is essential**: Every sustainable program has deep institutional buy-in. Programs that operate as add-ons get disrupted by transfers, lockdowns, and staffing changes.

7. **Allow re-entry after failure**: Best practices emphasize giving dropouts second chances. Program failure can increase motivation for future success.

8. **Measure beyond recidivism**: Use validated pre/post instruments (CERQ, TMMS-24, RSES) plus portfolio-based competency demonstration. Track employment, housing, family reunification, and educational attainment.

9. **The mentor development pipeline takes 6-18 months minimum**: Hope for Prisoners requires 12 months before mentornship training. OMCP requires ~1 year of education. Quick-to-mentor models like RSVP rely on immersive daily contact rather than formal training.

10. **Language and identity matter**: Defy's insistence on "Entrepreneurs-in-Training" rather than "inmates" reflects a broader pattern -- successful programs create new identities and language that participants adopt as part of their transformation.
