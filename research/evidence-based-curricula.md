## 1. Cognitive Behavioral Interventions

Cognitive behavioral interventions (CBIs) are the most extensively validated class of correctional programming. Research consistently shows that CBT-based programs reduce recidivism among moderate- and high-risk offenders. Three major programs dominate the field.

### 1.1 Thinking for a Change (T4C)

**Developer:** National Institute of Corrections (NIC), U.S. Department of Justice

**Evidence Rating:** Promising (CrimeSolutions.ojp.gov, based on one study)

**Curriculum Structure:**
- 25 lessons, each lasting 1-2 hours
- Delivered to small groups of 8-12 participants
- Typical program length: 12-30 weeks depending on session frequency
- Free scripted manual available through NIC

**Three Core Components:**

1. **Cognitive Self-Change** — Teaches participants a thorough process for self-reflection focused on uncovering antisocial thoughts, feelings, attitudes, and beliefs. Participants learn to identify "thinking errors" and develop cognitive restructuring skills.

2. **Social Skills Development** — Prepares participants to engage in prosocial interactions, with emphasis on understanding how their actions affect others. Skills are practiced through role-play and rehearsal.

3. **Problem-Solving Skills** — Integrates cognitive restructuring and social skills into a structured problem-solving framework for handling challenges and stressful situations.

**Sample Lesson Topics:**
- Active Listening
- Thinking Controls Our Behavior
- Recognizing Risk Thinking
- Understanding Others' Feelings
- Responding to Anger
- Problem Solving Steps
- "State the Problem"

**Session Format:** Each session combines didactic instruction, role-play demonstrations, lesson review, and homework assignments.

**Facilitator Requirements:**
- NIC offers free training through two tracks: Basic Facilitator Training and Advanced Practicum (Train the Trainer)
- Facilitators receive a scripted manual with explicit content and objectives for each lesson
- No specific degree requirement listed, but training through NIC is mandatory

**Evidence of Effectiveness:**
- A 2009 evaluation found that 23% of T4C participants recidivated compared to 36% in the control group during a 6-month follow-up period — a statistically significant reduction
- Serves both adults and juveniles in probation, incarceration, and aftercare settings
- Effectiveness research to date has focused primarily on adult probationers

**Relevance to Forge:** T4C's structured, manualized format and free availability through NIC make it an ideal foundational curriculum. Its three-component model (cognitive restructuring + social skills + problem solving) maps well to mentorship development goals.

### 1.2 Moral Reconation Therapy (MRT)

**Developer:** Correctional Counseling, Inc. (Gregory Little and Kenneth Robinson, 1985)

**Theoretical Foundation:** Based on Lawrence Kohlberg's theory of moral development stages. MRT is a cognitive-behavioral treatment system focused on enhancing moral reasoning and decision-making.

**Curriculum Structure:**
- Structured around progressive steps (12 steps) that move participants through stages of moral reasoning
- Open-ended group format — participants enter at any time and progress at their own pace
- Workbook-driven: participants complete exercises and present work to group for feedback
- Groups typically meet 1-2 times per week
- Typical completion time: 3-6 months

**The 12 Steps of MRT:**
1. Confrontation of beliefs and behaviors
2. Assessment of current relationships
3. Reinforcement of positive behavior and habits
4. Positive identity formation
5. Enhancement of self-concept
6. Decrease in hedonism and development of frustration tolerance
7. Development of higher stages of moral reasoning
8-12. Progressive moral development exercises building toward autonomous moral reasoning

**Topics and Issue Areas Addressed:**
- Substance abuse and addiction
- Criminal thinking and behavior patterns
- Trauma (including veteran-specific trauma)
- Domestic violence
- Anger management and mental health
- Parenting and family dynamics
- Life skills (job readiness, money management, coping strategies)
- Sex offender treatment
- Juvenile delinquency

**Facilitator Requirements:**
- 4-day (32-hour) facilitator training required
- Only trained professionals are permitted to purchase MRT materials and run groups
- Training ensures program fidelity and participant success rates
- Materials must be purchased through Correctional Counseling, Inc. or Eagle Wing Books, Inc.

**Evidence of Effectiveness:**
- Over 200 published outcome studies have documented that MRT-treated offenders show significantly lower recidivism for periods as long as 20 years
- Widely implemented across U.S. corrections systems, community corrections, and treatment facilities
- Listed on SAMHSA's National Registry of Evidence-Based Programs and Practices (NREPP)

**Contact:** Correctional Counseling, Inc., phone (901) 360-1564

**Relevance to Forge:** MRT's step-based progression model and emphasis on moral development align well with mentorship program goals. The open-ended group format allows ongoing enrollment. However, materials are proprietary and require purchase, unlike T4C.

### 1.3 Reasoning and Rehabilitation (R&R)

**Developers:** Robert Ross and Elizabeth Fabiano (1985), developed in Canada

**Curriculum Structure:**
- 35 structured sessions (later revised to R&R2 with a shorter format)
- Sessions are approximately 2 hours each
- Delivered to groups of 6-8 participants
- Program duration: approximately 8-12 weeks (when sessions are held multiple times per week)

**Core Modules:**
1. **Self-Control and Self-Management** — Impulse control, "stop and think" techniques
2. **Interpersonal Problem Solving** — Systematic approaches to identifying and resolving problems
3. **Social Perspective Taking** — Understanding others' viewpoints and feelings
4. **Critical Reasoning** — Evaluating information logically, avoiding cognitive distortions
5. **Values Enhancement** — Examining and developing prosocial values
6. **Emotional Management** — Recognizing and regulating emotions
7. **Creative Thinking** — Generating alternative solutions
8. **Social Skills** — Practical skills for prosocial interaction
9. **Negotiation Skills** — Conflict resolution and assertiveness

**R&R2 (Revised Version):**
- Shorter format: 16 sessions
- Developed for broader implementation
- Includes adaptations for specific populations (e.g., R&R2 for ADHD, R&R2 for youths)
- Incorporates updated neuroscience on executive functioning

**Facilitator Requirements:**
- Trained facilitators required
- Training provided through certified R&R trainers
- Facilitator manual included

**Evidence of Effectiveness:**
- Developed and initially validated in Canadian corrections
- Multiple international studies (UK, Spain, Australia, Scandinavia) have demonstrated effectiveness
- Meta-analyses indicate R&R reduces reoffending by approximately 14% compared to control groups
- Recognized as effective by the UK's What Works initiative
- One of the earliest cognitive skills programs developed specifically for offenders

**Relevance to Forge:** R&R's comprehensive skills coverage (9 modules) provides a useful template for a mentorship curriculum. The R&R2 shorter format (16 sessions) may be more practical. Its international evidence base adds credibility.

### 1.4 CBT Meta-Analysis Findings

Research from Prison Policy Initiative confirms that people participating in treatment combinations consisting of basic skills, vocational training, and cognitive behavioral treatment were more likely to reduce post-release reconvictions. The CrimeSolutions database (OJP) rates cognitive behavioral therapy for moderate- and high-risk adult offenders as an effective practice. Meta-analyses consistently show CBT programs reduce recidivism by 20-30% compared to control conditions.

## 2. Mentor Training Curricula

### 2.1 Elements of Effective Practice for Mentoring (MENTOR)

**Developer:** MENTOR: The National Mentoring Partnership (now in 5th edition)

**Framework Overview:**
- Foundational guide with over 35 years of development
- Contains 16 elements addressing program design, implementation, and evaluation
- Emphasizes that poorly implemented mentoring can be harmful: research suggests that when mentoring is done without proper structure, it can be more harmful to young people than having no mentor at all

**Core Areas Covered:**
- Youth and community values integration
- Inclusive and safe program environments
- Program design and evaluation strategies
- Mentor recruitment, screening, and selection
- Training requirements for mentors
- Matching mentors with mentees
- Monitoring and supporting matches
- Closure procedures

**Specialized Supplements Available:**
- Peer and group mentoring
- E-mentoring
- LGBTQ+ mentoring
- STEM mentoring
- Workplace mentoring
- Critical mentoring
- Disability inclusion

**Implementation:** Interactive website at eepm.mentoring.org provides tools, checklists, and detailed best practices. The framework is designed as flexible guidelines rather than prescriptive mandates, allowing programs to adapt to their unique contexts.

**Relevance to Forge:** The Elements of Effective Practice provide the gold standard framework for structuring any mentoring program, including peer mentoring behind bars. The peer mentoring supplement is directly applicable.

### 2.2 Peer Mentoring in Criminal Justice Settings

**Key Research Findings:**

Peer mentoring programs in corrections typically include these components:

**Mentor Selection Criteria (common across programs):**
- Minimum time served (often 2+ years at facility)
- Demonstrated behavioral record (no recent disciplinary infractions, typically clean record for 1-2 years)
- Completion of relevant programming (CBT, substance abuse treatment, etc.)
- Recommendation from staff/case managers
- Interview and screening process
- Willingness to commit to program requirements

**Typical Mentor Training Components:**
- Active listening and communication skills (8-16 hours)
- Motivational interviewing basics (8-12 hours)
- Boundary setting and ethical conduct (4-8 hours)
- Crisis recognition and referral protocols (4-8 hours)
- Cultural competency and sensitivity (4-8 hours)
- Understanding stages of change (4 hours)
- Confidentiality requirements and limitations (2-4 hours)
- Role-play and practice scenarios (ongoing)
- Total initial training: typically 40-80 hours before deployment

**Ongoing Supervision/Support Structures:**
- Regular supervision meetings (weekly or biweekly) with program staff
- Peer mentor support groups (monthly)
- Continuing education sessions
- Documentation and reporting requirements
- Annual recertification/refresher training

**Notable Peer Mentoring Programs in Corrections:**

1. **Inside-Out Prison Exchange Program**
   - Based in Ardmore, PA (501(c)(3) nonprofit)
   - Brings together incarcerated and university students for dialogue-based learning
   - Offers Training Institutes (virtual and in-person) for facilitators
   - Multiple training sessions scheduled throughout the year
   - Contact: trainings@inside-out-center.org, (215) 507-5703
   - Focus on questioning and discovery rather than prescriptive answers

2. **Ready4Work** (Public/Private Ventures)
   - Multi-site reentry mentoring initiative
   - Faith-based and community volunteer mentors paired with returning citizens
   - Structured training for mentors on reentry challenges
   - Combined mentoring with employment services
   - Participants with mentors showed significantly lower recidivism

3. **Prison Fellowship Reentry Programs**
   - Partners with faith-based organizations and local service agencies
   - Provides formerly incarcerated people with reentry assistance
   - Mentoring is combined with practical support services

4. **Center for Employment Opportunities (CEO)**
   - Reentry employment program with mentoring component
   - Participants were 52% more likely to be employed than comparison group at 12 months post-enrollment
   - Combines transitional jobs with life skills support

5. **CALPIA (California Prison Industry Authority)**
   - Includes peer mentoring alongside vocational training
   - Participants were significantly less likely to be arrested at one, two, and three years post-release

**Evidence of Mentoring Effectiveness:**
- Social support has important repercussions on physical health and success at reintegration
- Programs combining mentoring with employment services show strongest outcomes
- Structured mentoring reduces recidivism more effectively than informal support alone

### 2.3 Peer Mentor Certification Models

While no single national certification exists for incarcerated peer mentors, several models have emerged:

- **State DOC-specific certifications** — Many state departments of corrections have developed internal certification programs for peer mentors, typically requiring completion of specific training modules, supervised mentoring hours, and ongoing education
- **Peer Support Specialist certifications** — Some states allow incarcerated people to earn peer support specialist credentials (originally developed for behavioral health) that include mentoring competencies
- **Community health worker certifications** — Adapted for correctional settings, these provide training in health education, advocacy, and peer support

## 3. Restorative Justice Programs

### 3.1 Overview and Core Practices

Restorative justice (RJ) is an approach to justice that focuses on repairing harm caused by criminal behavior through cooperative processes involving victims, offenders, and community members. Three primary models are used in correctional settings:

**1. Victim-Offender Dialogue (VOD) / Victim-Offender Mediation (VOM)**
- Facilitated meetings between victims and offenders (either direct or surrogate)
- Structured preparation process for both parties (often months of preparation)
- Trained facilitators guide the dialogue
- Focus on understanding impact, accountability, and agreement on restitution
- Can occur at any point in the justice process, including post-conviction/during incarceration
- Programs exist in Texas (TDCJ Victim Services), Ohio, Minnesota, and other states

**2. Peacemaking/Sentencing Circles**
- Community-based process involving victim, offender, family, community members, and justice professionals
- Guided by a keeper/facilitator using a talking piece
- Focus on consensus-based outcomes for healing and accountability
- Adapted for in-prison use in some jurisdictions as healing circles
- Used extensively in Indigenous/tribal justice systems

**3. Family Group Conferencing**
- Brings together offender, victim, family members, and supporters
- Facilitated discussion of the offense and its impact
- Development of a plan for making amends
- Originally developed in New Zealand (Maori traditions)
- Adapted for prison settings as part of reentry planning

### 3.2 Prison-Based Restorative Justice Programs

**Notable Programs:**

- **Insight Prison Project** (San Quentin, California) — Offers victim-offender education groups, impact of crime classes, and restorative justice dialogue programs within the prison
- **Resolve to Stop the Violence Project (RSVP)** (San Francisco County Jail) — Combines restorative justice with violence prevention; showed significant reductions in violent incidents within the facility
- **Victim Impact Classes** — Used across multiple state DOCs (Texas, California, Oregon), these classes help offenders understand the impact of crime on victims, often using surrogate victim panels
- **Alternatives to Violence Project (AVP)** — Volunteer-led workshops inside prisons teaching conflict resolution, communication, and community building; operates in prisons across 30+ states and internationally
- **Impact Justice Restorative Justice Project** — Pre-charge diversion program focused on young people of color; transitioned to Equal Justice USA (EJUSA) in November 2023 with a "healing justice" focus

### 3.3 Restorative Justice Curriculum Components (Typical)

**Impact of Crime Curricula (commonly 12-20 sessions):**
- Understanding victimization and its effects
- Types of victims (primary, secondary, community)
- Empathy development exercises
- Personal accountability and responsibility
- Making amends and restitution planning
- Listening to victim/survivor panels
- Writing accountability letters
- Community impact assessment

**Restorative Justice Facilitation Training (for peer facilitators):**
- RJ philosophy and principles (8 hours)
- Circle keeping skills (16 hours)
- Conflict resolution and de-escalation (8 hours)
- Trauma awareness (8 hours)
- Practice facilitation under supervision (ongoing)
- Total: typically 40-60 hours

### 3.4 Evidence of Effectiveness

- Meta-analyses indicate restorative justice programs reduce recidivism compared to traditional justice processing, with stronger effects for violent offenses than property offenses
- Victim satisfaction rates are consistently higher in RJ processes compared to traditional court processes (typically 80-90% satisfaction)
- Offender accountability and compliance with agreements is generally higher in RJ than court-ordered restitution
- Cost savings through reduced incarceration and court processing
- Limitations: most studies focus on juvenile or community-based programs; less rigorous research on prison-based RJ specifically

## 4. Trauma-Informed Approaches

### 4.1 SAMHSA's Trauma-Informed Framework

**Source:** SAMHSA's Concept of Trauma and Guidance for a Trauma-Informed Approach (SMA14-4884)

**SAMHSA's Definition of Trauma (Three E's):**
Individual trauma results from an **Event** or set of circumstances that is **Experienced** as physically or emotionally harmful or life-threatening and that has lasting adverse **Effects** on functioning and mental, physical, social, emotional, or spiritual well-being.

**The Four R's of a Trauma-Informed Approach:**
1. **Realize** — Widespread impact of trauma and understand potential paths to recovery
2. **Recognize** — Signs and symptoms of trauma in clients, families, staff, and others
3. **Respond** — Integrate knowledge about trauma into policies, procedures, and practices
4. **Resist re-traumatization** — Actively work to prevent re-traumatization of clients and staff

**Six Key Principles:**
1. **Safety** — Physical and psychological safety for staff and the people they serve
2. **Trustworthiness and Transparency** — Operations and decisions conducted with transparency to build trust
3. **Peer Support** — Mutual self-help and support among individuals with lived experience
4. **Collaboration and Mutuality** — Leveling power differentials; everyone has a role
5. **Empowerment, Voice, and Choice** — Shared decision-making, individual strengths recognized
6. **Cultural, Historical, and Gender Issues** — Responsive to cultural, racial, ethnic, and gender identity needs; addresses historical trauma

**Ten Implementation Domains:**
1. Governance and leadership
2. Policy
3. Physical environment
4. Engagement and involvement
5. Cross-sector collaboration
6. Screening, assessment, and treatment services
7. Training and workforce development
8. Progress monitoring and quality assurance
9. Financing
10. Evaluation

**Application to Corrections:**
- Prevalence of trauma among incarcerated populations is extremely high (estimates of 75-90% of incarcerated people have experienced significant trauma)
- Correctional environments can be inherently re-traumatizing (strip searches, isolation, noise, lack of privacy, loss of autonomy)
- Trauma-informed corrections requires systemic changes to environment, staff training, and program delivery
- Staff wellness and secondary traumatic stress must also be addressed

### 4.2 Seeking Safety

**Developer:** Lisa Najavits, Ph.D.

**Program Overview:**
- Present-focused treatment for co-occurring trauma/PTSD and substance abuse
- Can be delivered in group or individual format
- Designed for flexibility — sessions can be conducted in any order
- Does not require clients to discuss specific trauma details (a critical advantage in correctional settings where safety concerns exist)

**Curriculum Structure — 25 Topics organized in four content areas:**

**Cognitive Topics:**
1. Safety
2. PTSD: Taking Back Your Power
3. When Substances Control You
4. Honesty
5. Setting Boundaries in Relationships
6. Compassion
7. Creating Meaning
8. Discovery
9. Integrating the Split Self
10. Recovery Thinking

**Behavioral Topics:**
11. Taking Good Care of Yourself
12. Red and Green Flags
13. Asking for Help
14. Undoing the Knots
15. Coping with Triggers
16. Respecting Your Time
17. Self-Nurturing

**Interpersonal Topics:**
18. Healing from Anger
19. Getting Others to Support Your Recovery
20. Healthy Relationships
21. Community Resources

**Combination Topics:**
22. Commitment
23. Life Choices Game
24. Termination
25. Check-in (flexible topic)

**Session Format (typical 50-90 minutes):**
1. Check-in (how are you doing, any safety issues)
2. Quotation — inspirational quote related to topic
3. Relate topic to participants' lives
4. Handouts and discussion
5. Check-out (one thing learned, commitment for the week)

**Facilitator Requirements:**
- No specific degree requirement (can be delivered by paraprofessionals)
- Training is recommended but the manual is designed to be self-instructional
- Available in a published manual format

**Evidence of Effectiveness:**
- Over 40 published outcome studies
- Listed on multiple evidence-based registries (SAMHSA's NREPP, CEBC, etc.)
- Demonstrated effectiveness across diverse populations including incarcerated women, veterans, homeless individuals, and adolescents
- Studies show reductions in substance use, trauma symptoms, and related problems
- Has been implemented in federal and state prisons, jails, drug courts, and community corrections
- Particularly effective for women in corrections (high co-occurrence of trauma and substance use)

**Relevance to Forge:** Seeking Safety's flexibility (sessions in any order, no requirement to discuss specific trauma), its usability by paraprofessionals (peer mentors could be trained to co-facilitate), and its extensive corrections track record make it highly relevant.

### 4.3 Additional Trauma-Informed Curricula for Corrections

**Beyond Violence** (Stephanie Covington)
- Designed specifically for incarcerated women with histories of aggression/violence
- 20 sessions addressing trauma, relationships, and coping
- Gender-responsive and trauma-informed

- 6-session psychoeducational curriculum
- Designed for women in corrections and community settings
- Covers: understanding trauma, the connection between trauma and substance abuse, coping skills

- 11 sessions
- Addresses trauma's impact on the body, mind, spirit, and relationships
- Designed for women in residential and outpatient settings

**Trauma Recovery and Empowerment Model (TREM)**
- 24-29 session group intervention
- Designed for women with co-occurring trauma and substance use disorders
- Covers sexual abuse, physical abuse, emotional abuse recovery
- Has been adapted for correctional settings

**SAMHSA TIP 57: Trauma-Informed Care in Behavioral Health Services**
- Comprehensive clinical guide for integrating trauma-informed practices
- Available through NCBI Bookshelf
- Includes assessment tools, treatment protocols, and organizational change guidance
- Notes that individuals who have experienced repeated, chronic, or multiple traumas are more likely to exhibit pronounced symptoms including substance abuse, mental illness, and health problems

## 5. Life Skills and Reentry Curricula

### 5.1 Common Life Skills Program Areas

Prison-based life skills programs typically cover the following curriculum areas:

**Financial Literacy:**
- Budgeting and money management
- Banking and credit basics
- Debt management and financial planning
- Understanding pay stubs, taxes, and benefits
- Avoiding predatory lending and scams
- Savings strategies
- Common curricula: FDIC's Money Smart, National Endowment for Financial Education (NEFE) materials

**Employment and Career Readiness:**
- Resume writing and job applications
- Interview skills and professional communication
- Workplace behavior and expectations
- Career planning and goal setting
- Understanding employment rights and barriers
- Vocational training (welding, carpentry, plumbing, etc.)
- CrimeSolutions.ojp.gov rates corrections-based vocational training as "Promising" — participants had 28% higher odds of obtaining employment post-release

**Communication and Interpersonal Skills:**
- Active listening
- Assertive communication (vs. aggressive/passive)
- Giving and receiving feedback
- Professional communication (emails, phone calls)
- Public speaking basics
- Conflict resolution and negotiation

**Emotional Intelligence:**
- Self-awareness and self-regulation
- Empathy and social awareness
- Relationship management
- Stress management and coping skills
- Recognizing and managing emotions

**Leadership Development:**
- Personal responsibility and accountability
- Decision-making frameworks
- Goal setting and action planning
- Mentoring and coaching others
- Community engagement and civic responsibility

**Health and Wellness:**
- Physical health and nutrition
- Mental health awareness and self-care
- Substance abuse prevention and recovery
- Healthy relationships and family dynamics
- Parenting skills (BOP mandates parenting classes)

**Reentry Planning:**
- Housing: finding and maintaining stable housing
- Identification documents and records
- Transportation planning
- Healthcare access and enrollment
- Community resources and support networks
- Legal obligations (supervision requirements, restitution, etc.)

### 5.2 Notable Life Skills / Reentry Curricula

**Federal Bureau of Prisons (BOP) Programs:**
- Mandatory literacy: Inmates without high school diplomas/GEDs must complete a minimum 240-hour literacy program or obtain their GED
- ESL training for non-English speakers
- Parenting classes
- Wellness education
- Adult continuing education
- Vocational/occupational training tailored to labor market conditions
- Federal Prison Industries (UNICOR) work assignments
- Post-secondary education (self-funded)

**Integrated Reentry and Employment Strategies (IRES):**
- Pilot initiative (CSG Justice Center, 2017-2018)
- Tested in Milwaukee County, WI and Palm Beach County, FL
- Served 521 participants
- Focused on connecting people leaving incarceration to reentry and employment services at the right intensity and timing
- Found that jurisdictions frequently lack adequate resources and coordination to link returning individuals with necessary services

**Cognitive-Based Life Skills Programs:**
Research demonstrates that combining basic skills, vocational training, and cognitive behavioral treatment produces the strongest outcomes for reducing post-release reconvictions. Programs that integrate multiple modalities (education + employment + CBT) outperform single-focus interventions.

### 5.3 Key Barriers to Reentry Success

- Unemployment rates among formerly incarcerated people reach 27%
- Homelessness affects approximately 5,700 per 100,000 formerly incarcerated individuals
- Poverty is identified as the strongest predictor for recidivism
- 68% of people released in 2005 were re-arrested within 3 years
- 46-49% of released prisoners returned to prison within 5 years

## 6. RAND Corporation Research

### 6.1 Major Study: Evaluating the Effectiveness of Correctional Education (2013)

**Publication:** RAND Research Report RR-564 (Davis, Bozick, Steele, Saunders, Miles)

**Study Scope:** Meta-analysis of correctional education programs, examining studies from 1980-2011.

**Key Findings:**

**Recidivism Reduction:**
- Inmates who participate in correctional education programs have **43% lower odds of recidivating** compared to those who do not participate
- Correctional education reduces reincarceration risk by approximately **13 percentage points**

**Employment Outcomes:**
- Participants had **13% higher odds of obtaining employment** post-release compared to non-participants
- Applicants with prison-earned bachelor's degrees showed **42% higher callback odds** versus those with only GEDs (2024 experimental audit study)

**Cost-Benefit Analysis:**
- For every **$1 spent** on correctional education, **$5 is saved** on reincarceration costs
- Annual incarceration costs (approximately $92,000 in Massachusetts) far exceed even expensive college program costs
- Vera Institute (2019) estimated that expanding postsecondary education would reduce incarceration costs by **$365.8 million annually** across states

**By Education Type:**
- **Vocational education:** 15.6% decrease in recidivism odds
- **College/postsecondary programs:** Described as especially effective tools for decreasing recidivism
- **GED programs:** Significantly lowered recidivism rates, though less than 10% of in-prison GED earners go on to take any college coursework
- **Adult Basic Education (ABE):** Documented positive outcomes
- In-prison GED programs without supplemental educational experiences are insufficient to prepare students for further education

**Historical Context:**
- Approximately 350 college programs operated in prisons in the early 1990s
- Following the 1994 crime bill's elimination of Pell Grant eligibility, that number dropped to about 12 by 2005
- Nearly 73% of formerly incarcerated people with GED credentials earned them while incarcerated

### 6.2 Correctional Education as Deterrence Context

NIJ research shows that the certainty of being caught is a vastly more powerful deterrent than punishment severity. Prisons may actually exacerbate recidivism through exposure to criminal networks. Individuals naturally reduce criminal activity with age, particularly around age 35, meaning lengthy sentences for aging offenders represent costly incapacitation rather than effective deterrence. This underscores the importance of rehabilitation programming over purely punitive approaches.

### 6.3 RAND Recommendations

- Invest in correctional education as a cost-effective recidivism reduction strategy
- Prioritize college-level and vocational education programs
- Supplement GED programs with additional educational opportunities
- Integrate education with employment services for reentry
- Conduct more rigorous research on computer-assisted instruction in corrections
- Address barriers to implementation (funding, staffing, technology access)

- Transition and reentry programming
- Community corrections

### 7.3 NIC Evidence-Based Practices Framework

NIC promotes adoption of evidence-based practices (EBP) across corrections, emphasizing:

- **Risk-Need-Responsivity (RNR) Principle:** Target services to higher-risk individuals, address criminogenic needs, and match intervention style to individual learning styles
- **Validated Risk/Needs Assessment Tools:** Systematic assessment of risk level and needs to guide programming decisions
- **Cognitive Behavioral Approaches:** Prioritizing interventions that address criminal thinking patterns
- **Motivational Enhancement:** Using motivational interviewing and stages of change to engage participants
- **Community Partnerships:** Building connections between institutions and community services for continuity of care
- **Quality Assurance:** Monitoring program fidelity and measuring outcomes

## 8. Implications for Forge Program Design

### 8.1 Recommended Curriculum Framework

Based on this research, the Forge mentorship development program should consider a multi-component curriculum incorporating:

**Foundation Layer — Cognitive Behavioral Skills (drawn from T4C and R&R):**
- Cognitive restructuring / thinking error identification
- Problem-solving skills
- Social skills and perspective-taking
- Emotional regulation
- Recommended: 20-25 structured sessions

**Mentor Development Layer:**
- Active listening and communication (adapted from T4C)
- Motivational interviewing basics
- Boundary setting and ethics
- Crisis recognition and referral
- Cultural competency
- Recommended: 40-80 hours of initial training

**Trauma-Informed Layer (drawn from Seeking Safety and SAMHSA):**
- Understanding trauma and its effects
- Coping skills and safety planning
- Self-care and secondary trauma awareness
- Creating psychologically safe environments
- Recommended: Integrated throughout, with 6-12 dedicated sessions

**Restorative Justice Layer:**
- Understanding impact of crime
- Empathy development
- Accountability practices
- Circle keeping skills
- Recommended: 12-16 sessions, plus ongoing circle practice

**Life Skills Layer:**
- Financial literacy
- Employment readiness
- Communication skills
- Health and wellness
- Reentry planning
- Recommended: 12-20 sessions, modular format

### 8.2 Program Structure Recommendations

- **Group size:** 8-12 participants (consistent across evidence-based programs)
- **Session length:** 1.5-2 hours
- **Session frequency:** 2-3 times per week
- **Total program length:** 6-12 months for full mentor development
- **Ongoing support:** Weekly supervision, monthly peer support groups, annual recertification

### 8.3 Mentor Selection Criteria (derived from research)

- Minimum 2 years at facility
- Clean disciplinary record for at least 1 year
- Completion of foundational cognitive behavioral programming
- Staff recommendation
- Demonstrated prosocial behavior and leadership potential
- Willingness to commit to full training and ongoing requirements
- Interview and screening process

### 8.4 Evidence-Based Design Principles

1. **Target moderate-to-high risk individuals** (RNR principle — low-risk individuals may be harmed by intensive programming)
2. **Address criminogenic needs** (criminal thinking, antisocial attitudes, substance abuse, antisocial peers)
3. **Use structured, manualized curricula** for program fidelity
4. **Integrate multiple modalities** (CBT + education + employment + mentoring produces strongest outcomes)
5. **Build in trauma-informed principles** throughout all programming
6. **Include restorative practices** for accountability and community building
7. **Provide ongoing supervision and support** for mentors
8. **Measure outcomes** systematically (recidivism, behavior, skill acquisition, program completion)
