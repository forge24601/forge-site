## 1. CDCR Innovative Programming Grants (IPG)

### Overview

The Innovative Programming Grants (IPG) are administered by CDCR's Division of Rehabilitative Programs (DRP) to fund nonprofit organizations providing rehabilitative programming inside California state prisons. The program began in 2015 and has funded the establishment of 299 programs since 2014.

**Source:** [CDCR DRP Grants Page](https://www.cdcr.ca.gov/rehabilitation/grants/)

### Funding Structure

- **Current cycle (2025-2028):** $4 million/year for 3 years = $12 million total
- **Previous cycle (2022-2025):** $4 million/year for 3 years = $12 million total
- **Historical:** Started with $2.5 million in one-year grants in 2015; $14.5 million awarded in 2016
- 25 organizations funded in the 2025-2028 cycle

**Source:** [IPG 2025-2028 Awards](https://www.cdcr.ca.gov/rehabilitation/innovative-programming-grant-ipg-2025%E2%80%912028-awards/)

### Types of Programs Funded

IPG funds an extraordinary range of programming, including:

**Trauma-Informed / Emotional Intelligence:**
- California Reentry Institute "SEED" — 37-lesson trauma and emotional intelligence curriculum (166.5 hours)
- Envisioning Justice Solutions "Exploring Trauma Plus (ET+)" — expressive arts and mindfulness
- Compassion Prison Project "Trauma Talks" — 12-part video series on Adverse Childhood Experiences with 2-hour weekly discussions
- Center for Gender & Justice "Beyond Trauma" — 12-session curriculum for women addressing complex childhood trauma

**Restorative Justice / Victim Impact:**
- Five Keys Schools "VOEG Program" — trauma-informed restorative justice
- Community Partners "Victim Awareness Program" — restorative justice dialogues with homicide survivors
- Prison of Peace — mediation and restorative justice training

**Arts-Based Programs:**
- Marin Shakespeare Company — anger management through performance + self-reflection through Shakespeare
- Old Globe Theatre "Reflecting Shakespeare" — mindfulness and social skills through theatrical performance
- Red Ladder Theatre "Art of Accountability" — improvisational theatre
- Prison Arts Collective — 60-hour facilitator training course plus apprenticeship
- San Diego State "Shared Horizons" — mural-making programs
- KALW "Uncuffed Music Radio" — 10-class, 20-hour music radio production workshop
- Jail Guitar Doors "Creative Expressions" — 8-week songwriting cycles (1.5 hours/week)

**Mindfulness / Yoga:**
- Prison Yoga + Meditation — trauma-informed yoga addressing PTSD
- Prison Yoga Project — yoga and mindfulness immersion for emotional regulation
- Boundless Freedom Project "Mindful Prisons" — 45-hour meditation/mindfulness cohorts

**Animal-Assisted Programs:**
- Marley's Mutts "Pawsitive Change" — 14-week dog training curriculum (20-25 participants/cycle)
- Paws For Life K9 Rescue — continuous year-round dog work (21 hours/week per participant)
- Little Angel's Service Dogs — 12-month dog training program
- Tender Loving Canines "POOCH" — service dog training for autism and veteran placement
- New Beginning at Circle T Ranch — 7-week equine-assisted learning with certification

**Reentry Preparation:**
- Root & Rebound "Roadmap to Reentry" — 12-lesson program covering employment, housing, family reintegration
- After Life Initiative "The Reentry Blueprint" — 11-week curriculum combining restorative justice with reentry prep
- Defy Ventures "CEO of Your New Life" — portfolio-building and business plan development

**Peer Education / Mentorship:**
- ReEvolution "Junior Mentors" — 44 new participants/year in intensive leadership program (14.5 hours/month)
- Timelist Group "Peer Support Group" — monthly groups (max 30 participants)
- C.R.O.P "Quest Program" — goal-setting with peer coaching (13-week cycles)

**Parenting:**
- Just Us 4 Youth "Restorative Parenting" — 16-week evidence-based parenting program
- Center for Restorative Justice Works "Get on the Bus" — annual family visitation program

**Academic / Humanities:**
- UC Santa Barbara "Foundations in the Humanities" — correspondence study in literary fiction (6 modules)
- Prison Education Project — weekly 7-week academic offerings creating "Prison-to-School Pipeline"

**Vocational:**
- Impact Justice "Growing Justice" — vertical farming vocational training
- Insight Garden Program — garden-based restorative training (48-week program)
- Thoroughbred Foundation "Second Chances Equine Care" — college-credit equine vocational courses

**Self-Directed:**
- Hustle 2.0 — 14-week, 80-hour in-cell self-guided CBT study

### Program Structure Patterns

Most IPG-funded programs share common structural elements:
- **Cohort sizes:** Typically 15-30 participants
- **Duration:** Ranges from 7 weeks to 12 months; most common is 8-16 weeks
- **Frequency:** Most meet 1-3 times per week for 1.5-3 hours per session
- **Cycles:** Programs typically run 2-4 cycles per year
- **Peer involvement:** Many programs incorporate peer facilitators or trained mentors

### Evaluation

No standardized outcome metrics are published across IPG programs. Individual programs may track their own outcomes, but CDCR does not publish consolidated evaluation data for the IPG portfolio.

## 2. Anti-Recidivism Coalition (ARC)

### Overview

Founded by Scott Budnick in 2013 in Los Angeles, ARC is one of California's most prominent reentry organizations. It employs nearly 200 staff, 80% of whom are formerly incarcerated. ARC uses a **credible messenger model** — formerly incarcerated individuals serve as mentors, facilitators, and advocates.

**Sources:** [ARC Website](https://antirecidivism.org/) | [ARC Inside Programs](https://antirecidivism.org/our-programs/inside/)

### In-Prison Programming: Hope & Redemption Team (HART)

The HART program is the core of ARC's in-prison work:

**History:**
- Created in 2017 by Sam Lewis (formerly incarcerated lifer)
- Originally deployed to 7 prisons
- Expanded to 29 prisons by 2020, now in 33 prisons (including women's facilities CIW, CCWF, and Sacramento CCTRP)
- Now a statewide CDCR program offered in every prison

**Structure:**
- **Duration:** 10-week workshops
- **Frequency:** 4 days/week, 6 hours/day (three 2-hour groups per day)
- **Cycles:** 4 cycles per year (running continuously)
- **5th day:** Reserved for administrative work — case management, evaluation, data tracking, course preparation
- **Facilitators:** All formerly incarcerated individuals, many returning to the very prisons they paroled from

**Curriculum — Three Core Areas:**
1. **AVATAR Parole Preparation and Relapse Prevention** — practical reentry planning, parole readiness, strategies for avoiding relapse into criminal behavior
2. **Criminals and Gang Members Anonymous (CGA)** — a 12-step recovery program modeled on AA, established in the 1990s, addressing "criminal lifestyle addiction" through spiritual transformation and peer accountability
3. **Emotional Intelligence** — developing self-awareness, emotional regulation, and interpersonal skills

### Mentorship Model

- **Credible Messenger Model:** Formerly incarcerated mentors connect on a deeper level with participants by sharing their own transformation journeys
- **Transformative Mentoring:** Staff and members are trained in this methodology
- **Peer-to-peer:** Mentors encourage incarcerated individuals to believe in themselves and pursue education
- **Continuity:** Participants can join ARC as members after release for continued reentry services

### Additional Programs

- **Credible Messengers Program** (launched 2023): Formerly incarcerated mentors in LA County juvenile facilities providing daily groups and individual mentoring
- **Ride Home Program:** Immediate post-release transportation and support
- **Ventura Training Center:** Dedicated facility for programming
- **Barber Academy:** Launching at Valley State Prison
- **EV Training Program:** Electric vehicle repair training for formerly incarcerated participants
- **Felony Expungement Services:** Managed by formerly incarcerated staff

### Outcomes

- HART evaluations indicate reduced violent incidents among participants
- Improved relationships by 13% among participants
- Increased self-esteem by 11% among participants
- Specific recidivism data not publicly available in detail

## 3. Insight Prison Project / GRIP (Guiding Rage Into Power)

### Overview

GRIP was developed by Jacques Verduin and originated at San Quentin State Prison. It is now administered by the GRIP Training Institute (separate from the Insight Prison Project, which rebranded as "insight-out"). GRIP is described as "an evidence-based methodology developed over 25 years of work with thousands of incarcerated people and many victims/survivors."

**Sources:** [GRIP Training Institute](https://grip-traininginstitute.org/) | [GRIP Program Page](https://grip-traininginstitute.org/grip/) | [CDCR BPH Program Description (PDF)](https://www.cdcr.ca.gov/bph/wp-content/uploads/sites/161/2023/05/GRIP-Program-Description-Public-2021-BOPH.pdf)

### Program Structure

- **Duration:** One full year (52 weeks)
- **Frequency:** Once per week, approximately 1-2 hours per class
- **Coursebook:** 215-page guided coursebook provided to all participants
- **Setting:** Primarily group-based classroom format inside prisons
- **Rooted in:** Restorative Justice principles, trauma-informed care, neuroscience research

### Curriculum Structure — Three Modalities

GRIP integrates three didactical modalities throughout the year:

1. **Instruction:** Teaching the theoretical framework — neuroscience of violence, emotional literacy, trauma, and behavioral patterns
2. **Process:** Exercises for working with deep emotional material that must be acknowledged, expressed, and integrated for insight and understanding to occur
3. **Practice:** Anchoring acquired insights into durable behavior through embodiment exercises

### Core Curriculum Topics

- **Emotional Literacy:** Understanding anger, rage, and underlying masked emotions (sadness, fear, shame); learning to identify and communicate unmet needs covered by rage
- **Body Awareness:** Tracking body signals that accompany strong emotions; learning to stop and discharge tension safely
- **Violence Origins:** Comprehending the origins of personal violence
- **Impulse Management:** Developing skills to track and manage strong impulses to avoid destructive acting-out
- **Mindfulness:** Cultivating present-moment awareness
- **Victim Impact:** Welcoming victims and survivors of crime to visit the class, share their stories, and answer questions from participants
- **Restorative Justice:** Understanding harm caused, taking accountability, and developing empathy

### Peer Education Model

A major distinguishing feature of GRIP is its peer education structure:
- Experienced students co-facilitate classes and mentor newer students
- All participants become fully engaged as integral stakeholders
- The program employs "normative culture" — students cultivate intrinsic motivation by being actively involved in setting and enforcing standards and norms integral to the course
- This creates ownership and accountability within the learning community

### Outcomes

- **Recidivism:** Since inception in 2012, 421 graduates have been released with only 2 returning to prison — an approximately 0.5% recidivism rate (compared to California's general 3-year recidivism rate of ~41.9%)
- **2025 Research:** A peer-reviewed study published in the *Journal of Community & Applied Social Psychology* (Calhoun, 2025) examined socio-emotional learning effects of GRIP as perceived by both participant and non-participant incarcerated persons

### Key Design Insights for Forge

- The yearlong duration allows for deep transformation rather than surface-level behavior change
- The peer co-facilitation model creates a pipeline of experienced mentors
- The "normative culture" approach gives participants ownership over program standards
- Victim/survivor panels create direct accountability and empathy development
- The three-modality approach (instruction, process, practice) ensures knowledge becomes embodied behavior

## 4. The Last Mile

### Overview

The Last Mile (TLM) is an Oakland-based 501(c)(3) that originated at San Quentin State Prison. It currently operates in 17 classrooms across 8 states. Founded by Chris Redlitz and Beverly Parenti.

**Sources:** [The Last Mile Website](https://thelastmile.org/about/) | [Press Kit](https://thelastmile.org/press-kit/)

### Program Structure

**Two Training Tracks:**
1. **Web Development** (primary track)
2. **Audio/Video Production**

**Duration:** Each track runs for 6 months (2 tracks = 1 full year for complete program)

**Schedule:** Students attend class at least 4 days/week, 8 hours/day. Each track consists of modules spanning 2-3 weeks each.

**Technology:** The Chromebook Pilot Program (launched 2021 at Pelican Bay) was the first of its kind in the U.S., providing students with mobile technology to carry between housing units and classrooms.

### Web Development Curriculum

The coding curriculum covers:
- HTML
- CSS
- JavaScript
- React
- Node.js
- Express
- MongoDB
- MySQL
- Git source control
- Soft skills and professional readiness

### Cohort Selection Process

**Application Requirements:**
- Essay questions
- Logic test
- In-person interview

**Eligibility:**
- High school diploma or equivalency
- At least 12 months and no more than 36 months remaining on sentence
- No infractions on record for prior 18 months
- No LWOP (Life Without Possibility of Parole) sentence

**Cohort Composition:**
- TLM ensures each cohort represents the facility population and demonstrates team-working ability
- Some inmates with life sentences serve as instructors for each cohort (creating a peer teaching pipeline)

### Post-Release Support

- TLM offers scholarships for returned citizens
- Employment placement support
- Professional readiness and networking

### Outcomes

- **Employment rate:** 75% of graduates who return to society secure employment
- **Scale:** 1,500+ student participants; 1,000+ returned citizens reintegrated
- Program reduces recidivism and taxpayer spending on incarceration (specific recidivism data not published)

### Key Design Insights for Forge

- The intensive daily schedule (8 hours/day, 4 days/week) mirrors real workplace expectations
- Using lifers as instructors creates sustainable peer teaching
- Rigorous selection (essays, logic tests, interviews) ensures commitment
- The 12-36 month sentence window targets those close enough to release to apply skills immediately

## 5. Project Rebound (Cal State)

### Overview

Project Rebound is the nation's longest-running prison-to-college pipeline, originally founded in 1967 by John Irwin at San Francisco State University. It supports formerly incarcerated individuals enrolling and succeeding at California State University campuses.

**Source:** [CSU Project Rebound](https://www.calstate.edu/impact-of-the-csu/student-success/project-rebound)

### Program Structure

**Current Reach:** 19 CSU campuses

**Pre-Release / In-Prison Outreach:**
- Project Rebound staff volunteer time to teach classes inside correctional facilities
- Partnerships with community colleges (Laney College, Mount Tamalpais College)
- Connect with prospective students before they may be thinking about attending CSU
- CSU Fresno's IPG-funded outreach at Pleasant Valley State Prison: 12-lesson reentry program covering housing, benefits, employment, and civic reintegration

**Enrollment Support:**
- Admissions and enrollment assistance
- Academic and financial counseling
- Course planning and advising

**Wraparound Services:**
- Housing assistance (including the John Irwin House — first transformative housing community for formerly incarcerated university students in the U.S., established 2018)
- Employment support
- Mentorship from peers and staff (many of whom are formerly incarcerated)
- Mental health and wellness services

### Bridge Model

The program functions as a bridge at multiple stages:
1. **In-prison outreach** — classes and connection before release
2. **Pre-enrollment** — application assistance and counseling
3. **Active enrollment** — academic support and wraparound services
4. **Post-graduation** — employment placement and graduate school support

### Outcomes

- **GPA:** System-wide average of 3.0 since 2016
- **Recidivism:** 0% recidivism rate among Project Rebound students
- **Graduation rates:** 71% of transfer students graduate within 3 years; retention rates range 75-100% across campuses
- **Employment:** 87% of graduates secure full-time employment or admission to postgraduate programs
- **Comparison:** California's general 3-year recidivism rate is 41.9%

### Key Design Insights for Forge

- The 0% recidivism rate demonstrates the power of meaningful post-release engagement
- In-prison outreach before release builds connection and hope
- Peer mentorship from formerly incarcerated staff creates credible messenger relationships
- Housing support (John Irwin House) addresses a critical reentry barrier
- The program spans the full continuum: in-prison to enrolled to graduated to employed

## 6. Offender Mentor Certification Program (OMCP)

### Overview

The OMCP (now also called Occupational Mentor Certification Program) trains incarcerated individuals to become licensed substance abuse treatment counselors. It began at Solano County State Prison in 2009 and has expanded to multiple facilities.

**Sources:** [CDCR OMCP Page](https://www.cdcr.ca.gov/rehabilitation/omcp/) | [Inside CDCR — OMCP](https://www.cdcr.ca.gov/insidecdcr/2014/04/30/offender-mentor-certification-teaches-inmates-to-help-themselves-others/)

### Eligibility Requirements

- More than 5 years remaining on sentence (including life sentences)
- No pending or adjudicated-guilty serious Rules Violation Reports (RVRs) in the last 2 years
- No Restricted Housing Unit terms in the last 3 years
- Minimum 9th grade reading level
- Case-by-case reviews apply for violations outside these strict timeframes

### Application Process

- Submit application packet to institution's Correctional Counselor
- Participate in an interview
- Selected based on qualifications and institutional needs

### Training Structure

**Phase 1 — Education (approximately 1 year):**
- Classroom education in Substance Use Disorder (SUD) programming
- Topics include: substance abuse, self-esteem, denial management, character development
- Parallel process: candidates must address their own issues before helping others

**Phase 2 — Certification:**
- Pass the California Association of Alcohol and Drug Abuse Counselors (CAADAC) written examination
- Obtain AOD (Alcohol and Other Drug) certification from a Department of Health Care Services-recognized organization

**Phase 3 — Internship:**
- Complete a closely supervised 4,000-hour counseling internship
- Work within in-prison substance abuse treatment programs
- Work alongside certified contracted drug and alcohol counselors

**Post-Certification:**
- Assigned and paid as co-facilitators in DRP's Cognitive Behavioral Interventions (CBI) programs throughout all adult institutions
- Earn milestone credit and educational merit credit
- Certification is portable — can be used for employment upon release

### Program Locations

Operates across seven or more California facilities including:
- Solano State Prison (original site)
- Central California Women's Facility
- Valley State Prison
- Avenal State Prison
- California Men's Colony
- Additional expanding locations

### Outcomes

- Graduates have gone on to earn bachelor's and master's degrees
- Multiple graduates working as substance abuse counselors post-release
- Specific recidivism data for OMCP graduates not publicly available

### Key Design Insights for Forge

- The requirement that candidates address their own issues first ("parallel process") is a powerful design principle
- 4,000 internship hours create deep competency and investment
- Portable professional certification provides real post-release employment value
- Targeting long-term/life-sentenced individuals creates a stable mentor workforce
- Paid co-facilitator roles provide meaningful purpose and institutional value

## 7. CDCR Peer Mentoring / Peer Education Programs

### Overview

Under the California Model (Governor Newsom's framework for prison transformation), peer mentorship is one of four pillars alongside normalization, dynamic security, and trauma-informed care. CDCR operates several peer mentoring programs across its institutions.

**Source:** [California Model — Peer Mentorship](https://www.cdcr.ca.gov/insidecdcr/2023/12/11/california-model-pillar-peer-mentorship/)

### Peer Literacy Mentor Program (PLMP)

**Purpose:** Provide literacy services for incarcerated adults without a high school diploma or equivalency

**Structure:**
- 20 trained mentors per site tutor and support students from beginning literacy through 12th grade/GED
- Mentors must already have a high school diploma, GED, and/or college degree
- Mentors apply, are interviewed, and selected based on abilities

**Mentor Training — Three Modules:**
1. Lesson development and instructional strategies
2. Instructional strategies and criminal thinking
3. (Third module content not specified in available sources)

**Source:** [CMF Peer Literacy Mentor Program](https://www.cdcr.ca.gov/insidecdcr/2023/05/09/cmf-peer-literacy-mentor-program-makes-a-difference/)

### Youth Peer Mentor Program

**Training Requirements:**
- More than 100 hours of classroom instruction
- Additional independent study hours
- 160-hour certification requirement through practicum hours

**Curriculum Topics:**
- Denial Management
- Criminal Thinking
- 12-Step Orientation
- Relapse Prevention
- Trauma-Informed Care
- Motivational Interviewing
- Case Management
- Youth Mental Health First Aid Certification

**Post-Training Role:**
- Co-facilitating groups
- One-on-one peer support

**Source:** [Youth Peer Mentor Graduates](https://www.cdcr.ca.gov/insidecdcr/2019/10/08/youth-peer-mentor-program-graduates-aim-to-help-others/)

### Youth Offender Rehabilitative Community (YORC)

**Location:** Valley State Prison (model site)

**Structure:**
- Peer mentorship initiatives where individuals progress from mentee to mentor
- Complementary programming includes art, music, media production, and equine therapy
- Trauma-informed approach addressing root causes of criminal behavior
- Creates "a safer community because of (being relatable), the conversations that we're able to have with staff"

### San Quentin Peer Mentoring

**Scale:** More than 40 mentors for approximately 130 mentees (roughly 1:3 ratio)

**Programs utilizing peer mentors:**
- Youth Offender
- Offender Mentor Certification (OMCP)
- Peer Support Specialist
- Peer Literacy Mentor
- Incarcerated Persons Advisory Council
- The People in Blue
- Various orientation and pre-release programs

**Mentor Assignments:**
- Each mentor assigned up to 3 mentees
- Responsibilities include: orientations, annual/semi-annual check-ins, pre-release preparation

### Key Design Insights for Forge

- The mentee-to-mentor progression pipeline is a core design pattern across CDCR
- 100+ hour training requirements create serious, credentialed mentors
- The 1:3 mentor-to-mentee ratio at San Quentin is a useful benchmark
- Multiple certification pathways (Youth Mental Health First Aid, Motivational Interviewing) add professional value
- Peer mentorship has been elevated to a system-wide pillar under the California Model

## 8. San Quentin Programs

### Overview

San Quentin State Prison (now San Quentin Rehabilitation Center) has been the flagship site for California's prison programming for decades. Under Governor Newsom's California Model, it is undergoing a major transformation with the opening of an 80,000 sq ft Learning Center in February 2026.

**Sources:** [San Quentin Learning Center](https://www.cdcr.ca.gov/the-california-model/san-quentin-learning-center/) | [Governor Newsom Announcement](https://www.gov.ca.gov/2026/02/20/governor-newsom-transforms-san-quentin-opens-nation-leading-learning-center/)

### San Quentin Learning Center (Opened February 2026)

**Physical Structure — Four Buildings:**
- **Building A:** Technology and media center — coding classrooms, podcast studios, multimedia production, general classrooms, ground-level reentry center
- **Building B:** Library, classrooms, administrative offices
- **Building C:** Multipurpose gathering space, cafe, store, general classrooms, outdoor learning access
- **Building D:** Security processing for staff and volunteers

**Design Philosophy:** "Normalization" — bringing life in prison as close as possible to life outside to facilitate community adjustment upon release

### Mount Tamalpais College (formerly Prison University Project)

**History:**
- Founded in fall 1996 with two volunteer instructors, donated textbooks, and no budget
- Prison University Project incorporated in 2003
- Renamed Mount Tamalpais College in 2020
- Received National Humanities Medal from President Obama in 2016
- Achieved independent accreditation from ACCJC

**Program Structure:**
- ~300 students enrolled per semester
- 20 courses offered per semester
- Most courses worth 3 credits; 60 credits required for Associate of Arts degree
- Non-credit preparatory courses in Mathematics and English for those needing them
- Credit courses eligible for transfer to any CSU or UC campus
- Graduates receive priority admission to any of the 23 CSU campuses

**Curriculum:**
- Associate of Arts in Liberal Arts / General Studies
- Courses include: Calculus, Biology, Environmental Science, Chemistry, English, Composition
- Multi-disciplinary: humanities, social sciences, math, science
- Faculty use same syllabi and expectations as outside colleges
- Trauma-informed pedagogical approach with specific learning supports

**Scale:**
- Nearly 5,000 students have taken at least one course since 1996
- ~150 associate's degrees awarded through initial partnership with Patten University
- Funded entirely through private donations (historically)

**Source:** [Mount Tamalpais College](https://www.mttamcollege.edu/)

### Cal State LA Prison Graduation Initiative

- Expanding bachelor's degree program to San Quentin Rehabilitation Center
- Partnership with Cal State East Bay and San Francisco State
- Inaugural cohort of 35 incarcerated students starting fall 2026
- First cohort expected to graduate by spring 2028

**Source:** [Cal State LA Announcement](https://news.calstatela.edu/2026/03/02/under-governor-newsoms-california-model-cal-state-la-expands-bachelors-degree-program-to-san-quentin-rehabilitation-center/)

### Other Notable San Quentin Programs

- **The Last Mile:** Coding and tech education (see Section 4)
- **GRIP (Guiding Rage Into Power):** Violence prevention (see Section 3)
- **Insight Garden Program:** 48-week mindfulness-based environmental education
- **San Quentin News:** Award-winning inmate newspaper
- **Ear Hustle:** Award-winning podcast produced by incarcerated individuals
- **Concrete Rose:** Truck driving vocational course
- **Adjustment Justice Career Center:** Career counseling and job placement
- **Multiple peer mentorship programs:** 40+ mentors serving ~130 mentees

### Key Design Insights for Forge

- San Quentin demonstrates that concentrating high-quality programs creates a transformative ecosystem
- The normalization philosophy (making prison life resemble outside life) is a deliberate design choice
- Accredited college courses with real transfer credit provide tangible post-release value
- The variety of programs (academic, vocational, arts, therapeutic, mentorship) addresses different needs and learning styles
- Media programs (newspaper, podcast) give incarcerated individuals a public voice

## 9. Hustle 2.0 and Other Cognitive Behavioral Programs

### Hustle 2.0

**Overview:**
Hustle 2.0 is a trauma-responsive Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) program used in 800+ prisons and jails across 47 states, including multiple CDCR institutions. It is distinctive for being a self-guided, in-cell program.

**Sources:** [Hustle 2.0 Website](https://www.hustle20.com/) | [CoreCivic Profile](https://www.corecivic.com/news/hustle-2.0-maximizes-potential-and-rehabilitation-for-incarcerated-individuals)

**Program Structure:**
- **Format:** Self-directed, in-cell workbook program
- **Three Workbooks:**
  1. The Preseason
  2. The Hustle Guide: Book One
  3. The Hustle Guide: Book Two
- **Total content:** 1,266+ pages of homework assignments, workbooks, case studies, journaling prompts, and exercises
- **32 courses** across the three workbooks
- **Interaction:** Primarily through mail correspondence; optional collaborative sessions with peers and housing unit staff
- **No waitlist:** Open enrollment for all interested participants
- **College credit:** Completers of all three books earn college credit from Southern Utah University

**Six Primary Learning Goals:**
1. Replacing criminality with prosocial thinking and behavior
2. Promoting positive relationships and community
3. Developing empathy and emotional awareness
4. Providing effective coping strategies
5. Building good habits, breaking bad habits
6. Preparing for reentry and post-release employment

**Curriculum Topics:**
- Victim awareness
- Changing criminal thinking
- Anger management
- Managing trauma
- Employment readiness
- Well-being
- Recovering from addictive behaviors
- Building and maintaining healthy relationships
- Exploring feelings
- Entrepreneurial mindset

**Development:**
- Co-authored by incarcerated individuals from Pelican Bay State Prison alongside rehabilitation experts
- Uses evidence-based, trauma-informed strategies
- Incorporates testimonials, case studies, art, and humor

**Outcomes:**
- 20,000+ men and women have graduated across all states
- 1,500+ participants since 2022 launch
- 91% of participants with violent misconduct records avoided further violence
- 93% had no further misconduct at all
- 82% of those in restrictive housing did not return after completing the program
- 85% less expensive than traditional group-facilitated CBT

**In CDCR specifically:** Listed as available at multiple institutions; funded through IPG (14-week, 80-hour in-cell study at Corcoran State Prison in 2022-2025 cycle)

### CDCR Cognitive Behavioral Interventions (CBI)

**Source:** [CDCR CBI Page](https://www.cdcr.ca.gov/rehabilitation/cbi/)

CDCR's own CBI programs are the system's primary therapeutic programming:

**CBI-SUD (Substance Use Disorder):**
- Eligibility: Minimum 6 months to release
- Intensive Outpatient: 2 hours/day, 5 days/week, approximately 14 weeks
- SUD curriculum deliverable in 16 weeks at standard pace (3 days/week, 2 hours/day)

**CBI-Life Skills:**
- Eligibility: Minimum 12 months to release
- Standard delivery: 3 days/week, 2 hours/day
- Duration: 22 weeks
- Topics: Treatment readiness, anger management, victim impact, criminal thinking, family planning/parenting

**CBI-SO (Sexual Offender):**
- 54 sessions split into 8 modules
- Complementary groups: Anger Management, Dialectical Behavioral Therapy (DBT), Criminal Thinking, Social Skills

**Aftercare:**
- Eligibility: 4+ months remaining after completing CBI
- One 90-minute session per week for 13 weeks
- Combines group instruction with independent materials

**Short-Term Education:**
- For those with 6 months or less remaining
- One mandatory educational session
- Supplementary resources and community service information

**Availability:** All CDCR institutions statewide

**Facilitators:** CBI programs use both contracted counselors and OMCP-certified incarcerated co-facilitators

### Key Design Insights for Forge

- Hustle 2.0 proves that self-directed, in-cell programming can be effective — useful for restricted populations or as supplemental material
- Co-authorship with incarcerated individuals increases program credibility and relevance
- The tiered CBI system (SUD, Life Skills, SO, Aftercare) shows how to structure programming for different needs and timelines
- The 16-22 week program lengths provide a useful benchmark for structured group CBT
- OMCP graduates serving as CBI co-facilitators demonstrates a functional peer-professional pipeline

## 10. Key Takeaways for Forge Program Design

### Common Structural Patterns Across Successful Programs

| Element | Range Found | Most Common |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Program Duration | 7 weeks to 1 year | 10-22 weeks for structured programs; 1 year for deep transformation |
| Session Frequency | 1-5 days/week | 2-4 days/week |
| Session Length | 1-2 hours per session | 2 hours |
| Cohort Size | 15-30 participants | 20-25 |
| Mentor-to-Mentee Ratio | 1:1 to 1:3 | 1:3 |
| Training Hours for Mentors | 100-4,000 hours | 100+ hours minimum for peer mentors |

### Mentor Selection Criteria (Composite)

Across programs, the following eligibility criteria are common:
- Clean disciplinary record (typically 18 months to 2 years without serious violations)
- Minimum education level (9th grade reading to GED/diploma)
- Demonstrated personal transformation and accountability
- Interview and/or application with essays
- For programs targeting those near release: 12-36 months remaining
- For mentor/facilitator programs: 5+ years remaining (including lifers)

### Effective Curriculum Design Principles

1. **Credible Messenger Model:** Formerly or currently incarcerated facilitators dramatically increase program engagement and trust (ARC, GRIP, OMCP)
2. **Peer Education Pipeline:** The mentee-to-mentor progression creates sustainability and institutional knowledge (GRIP, CDCR peer programs, TLM)
3. **Parallel Process:** Requiring facilitators to do their own inner work first ensures authenticity (OMCP)
4. **Multi-Modal Learning:** Combining instruction, emotional processing, and embodied practice produces durable change (GRIP's three modalities)
5. **Normative Culture:** Giving participants ownership over program standards and norms creates intrinsic motivation (GRIP)
6. **Portable Credentials:** Programs offering recognized certifications or college credit dramatically improve post-release outcomes (OMCP, Project Rebound, TLM, Hustle 2.0)
7. **Victim/Survivor Engagement:** Direct exposure to victim impact creates empathy that cannot be achieved through curriculum alone (GRIP, IPG victim impact programs)
8. **Trauma-Informed Foundation:** Nearly every successful program explicitly incorporates trauma-informed approaches
9. **Continuity Across the Wall:** Programs that bridge in-prison to post-release (ARC, Project Rebound, TLM) show the strongest outcomes
10. **Self-Directed Options:** In-cell workbook programs (Hustle 2.0) can reach populations excluded from group programming
